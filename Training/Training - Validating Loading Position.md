🚛 **Team Training Update – Validating Our Load Positioning Practices**

Team,

Over the past few weeks, I’ve shared several training messages explaining why it is critical to keep our loads as **low as possible** and why we should load the **upper-deck vehicle in the forward position whenever possible**. These techniques help us keep both engines — the heaviest part of the vehicles — positioned low and close to the truck chassis, reducing side-to-side movement and improving overall stability on the road.

To reinforce and validate this practice, I’m sharing guidance from a more authoritative source: the **Florida Commercial Driver’s License Manual**, Section **3.2.3 – Don’t Be Top-heavy**. This section directly supports the same principles we’ve been applying in our daily operations.

📘 **Florida CDL Manual – Section 3.2.3: “Don’t Be Top-heavy”**

- The height of the vehicle’s center of gravity is critical for safe handling.
- A high center of gravity makes a vehicle more likely to tip over, especially during curves or sudden swerves.
- It is essential to keep cargo as **low as possible**.
- The heaviest parts of the cargo should always be positioned **under the lighter parts**.

This confirms that the loading methods we’ve been discussing — keeping engines low, minimizing height, and choosing loading positions that lower the center of gravity — are not only good practice, but are also aligned with the safety standards taught in state-level commercial driver training.

Let’s continue applying these principles consistently to maintain stability, reduce risk, and keep every move safe for ourselves and our customers.

Safety first, every time. Let’s keep winning together.
